Beyond GNSS: Deep‑Space Navigation

Yajur Kumar July 3, 2026

Navigation in Deep Space

In space mission, every space vehicle must continuously determine its position and velocity with sufficient accuracy to execute critical manoeuvres and meet mission objectives. Close to Earth, this navigation function is served almost effortlessly by the GNSS whose medium‑Earth‑orbit constellations broadcast time‑stamped signals that a receiver can convert into a real‑time, three‑dimensional fix accurate to a few metres. As missions extend toward the Moon, Mars, and beyond, however, this familiar capability rapidly degrades.

Signals that are robust at geocentric distances of 20 000 km turns very feeble at the lunar distance of 400 000 km, and at interplanetary ranges the received power falls so far below the thermal noise floor that no practical receiver can recover a usable measurement. Deep‑space navigation therefore relies on a hierarchy of techniques, each suited to a distinct operational regime: pushing GNSS to its physical limits with specialised lunar receivers, exploiting the exquisite radiometric precision of Earth’s Deep Space Network (DSN), and ultimately reading natural beacons - millisecond pulsars - that gives out signal pulses steadily across the galaxy. This article examines that technological side, illustrating each method with mission‑derived data, rigorous link‑budget analyses, and the fundamental equations that govern them. Figure 1 summarizes the navigation architecture adopted across different mission regimes and illustrates the transition from Earth-dependent to autonomous navigation techniques.

Limitations of GNSS Receiver

A GNSS receiver solves for its position by intersecting spheres whose radii are the pseudoranges to at least four satellites. For satellite $i$, the basic observation equation is:

$P_i = \sqrt{ (x_r - x_s^{(i)})^2 + (y_r - y_s^{(i)})^2 + (z_r - z_s^{(i)})^2 } +\ c(\delta t_r - \delta t_s^{(i)}) +\ I_i +\ T_i +\ \epsilon_i,$

where $c$ is the speed of light, $\delta t_r$ the receiver clock offset (estimated as a fourth unknown), $\delta t_s^{(i)}$ the satellite clock correction, $I_i$ and $T_i$ ionospheric and tropospheric delays, and $\epsilon_i$ noise and multipath. The formulation works brilliantly when the spacecraft is bathed in the main lobe of the GNSS antennas - but those antennas are shaped to illuminate the surface of the Earth, not deep space.

GPS L1 C/A Link Budget at Earth’s Surface

Take the GPS L1 C/A signal (1575.42 MHz, wavelength $\lambda$ $\approx$ $0.1903\ \text{m}$). The free‑space path loss over a distance $d$ is:

$ \text{FSPL(dB)} = 20\log_{10}\left( \frac{4\pi d}{\lambda} \right). $

At the GPS satellite altitude of 20,200 km (the zenith distance to a user on the ground),

$ \text{FSPL} = 20\log_{10}\left( \frac{4\pi \times 2.02 \times 10^7}{0.1903} \right) \approx 182.5\ \text{dB}. $

Each GPS satellite transmits the L1 C/A signal with an effective isotropic radiated power (EIRP) of approximately 57 dBm (the IS‑GPS‑200 specification guarantees a minimum of 56.8 dBm for the L1 C/A signal at the edge of the Earth’s coverage). The isotropic received power at zenith is therefore:

$ P_{\text{r,iso}} = 57 - 182.5 = -125.5\ \text{dBm}. $

Real receivers use an antenna with a few dBi of gain; for a worst‑case 5° elevation angle the guaranteed minimum received power at the Earth’s surface is –128.5 dBm (–158.5 dBW). The equivalent isotropic power is $-128.5 - 3 = -131.5\ \text{dBm}$. The thermal noise power in the 2.046 MHz null‑to‑null bandwidth of the C/A code is:

$ P_n = kTB = 1.38 \times 10^{-23} \times 290 \times 2.046 \times 10^6 \approx -111.0\ \text{dBm}. $

Thus, before de‑spreading, the signal is buried 20.5 dB below the noise floor.

Processing gain arises from the ratio of the spread bandwidth to the data bandwidth. For GPS L1 C/A, the chip rate is 1.023 Mcps and the navigation data rate is 50 bps, so the maximum processing gain is:

$ G_{p,\text{max}} = 10\log_{10}\left( \frac{1.023 \times 10^6}{50} \right) \approx 43.1\ \text{dB}. $

A 1‑ms coherent integration uses only one full code period (1023 chips) and provides $10\log_{10}(1023) \approx 30.1\ \text{dB}$ of gain. Extending the coherent integration to 20 ms (a single data bit) recovers the full 43.1 dB, provided that the navigation data bits are known a priori, are wiped off using bit prediction, or a pilot signal is used. Applying the full processing gain to the worst‑case isotropic received power yields a post‑correlation signal‑to‑noise ratio:

$ \text{SNR}_{\text{post}} = -131.5 - (-111.0) + 43.1 \approx 22.6\ \text{dB}, $

a comfortable margin that ensures robust tracking.

Signal Degradation in Lunar Orbit

Fig. 2: GPS L1 C/A received isotropic power as a function of distance from Earth

At the Moon’s mean distance of 384 400 km, the path loss increases by

$ 20\log_{10}\left( \frac{384400}{20200} \right) \approx 25.5\ \text{dB}. $

An isotropic antenna would now see –157.0 dBm. Relative to the –111.0 dBm noise floor, the signal is 46 dB below thermal noise. Even after the 30.1 dB processing gain of a 1‑ms coherent integration, the SNR remains –15.9 dB, requiring substantial non‑coherent summation to extract. The challenge is severe, but not insurmountable: the signal is not “lost,” merely deeply hidden (this degradation is illustrated quantitatively in Figure 2, which compares received power with the thermal noise floor), and can be recovered through careful engineering.

Lunar GNSS: The LuGRE Experiment

The Lunar GNSS Receiver Experiment (LuGRE), a joint payload of NASA and the Italian Space Agency, demonstrated that GNSS signals can indeed be acquired and tracked at the Moon. Delivered to Mare Crisium by Firefly Aerospace’s Blue Ghost lander on 2 March 2025, LuGRE achieved the first GNSS position fix on the lunar surface at a distance of approximately 391 000 km from Earth. During transit it had already tracked GPS and Galileo signals at 200 000 km, shattering the previous high‑altitude record and culminating in a successful fix on the surface.

LuGRE Link Budget

LuGRE employed a dedicated 14 dBi L‑band patch antenna pointed toward Earth. The received power at the Moon becomes

$ P_{\text{r, LuGRE}} = -157.0 + 14 = -143.0\ \text{dBm}. $

Figure 3 compares the received signal levels for terrestrial GPS reception, lunar isotropic reception, the LuGRE configuration, and Mars opposition.

The post‑correlation SNR after 20 ms coherent integration (exploiting the full 43.1 dB processing gain) is

$ \text{SNR}_{\text{20 ms}} = -143.0 - (-111.0) + 43.1 = +11.1\ \text{dB}, $

which is already comfortable. LuGRE likely achieved long coherent integrations by using Galileo’s E1C pilot signal and by performing data bit wipe‑off for the GPS L1 C/A signal, although the precise receiver implementation has not yet been described in published technical papers.

Weak‑Signal Acquisition and Tracking

The received signal after down‑conversion is modelled as

$ s(t) = A C(t-\tau) D(t) \cos\big(2\pi (f_{IF}+f_d) t + \phi\big) + n(t), $

where $C$ is the spreading code, $D$ the navigation data, $f_{IF}$ the intermediate frequency, $f_d$ the unknown Doppler shift (up to several kHz), and $n(t)$ white Gaussian noise. The receiver searches a two‑dimensional grid of code delay $\tau$ and Doppler $f_d$. For the Moon, orbital dynamics aiding – an onboard propagator that constrains the expected line‑of‑sight velocity to within a few m s⁻¹ – reduces the search space by orders of magnitude. Once acquired, the receiver tracks the carrier with a phase‑locked loop and the code with a delay‑locked loop, and generates pseudoranges for a standard least‑squares navigation solution.

LuGRE’s on‑surface fix achieved an accuracy on the order of 100 m (preliminary public data; a rigorous error analysis has yet to be published). It is worth noting that the pseudorange‑based accuracy is limited by multipath and clock errors; future lunar receivers could exploit the carrier‑phase observables, which after ambiguity resolution can deliver centimetre‑level relative positioning.

But Why Lunar GNSS Cannot Scale to the Planets

Despite its success, LuGRE is fundamentally a cis‑lunar technique. The GNSS satellites remain in Earth orbit; their signals must travel from the Earth–Moon system. For a Mars‑bound vehicle at opposition (about 100 million km), the additional path loss relative to the Moon is $20\log_{10}(10^8 / 4 \times 10^5) \approx 48\ \text{dB}$, pushing the received isotropic power to roughly –205 dBm, 94 dB below the noise floor. No practical spacecraft antenna or receiver architecture can compensate for such a deficit. Deep‑space navigation must therefore rely on different physical principles.

Earth‑Based Radiometric Tracking: The Deep Space Network

For six decades, interplanetary missions have navigated using the Deep Space Network (DSN), a trio of 34‑m and 70‑m antennas in Goldstone (California), Madrid (Spain), and Canberra (Australia). The DSN provides two fundamental radiometric observables: Doppler shift for range‑rate, and round‑trip light‑time for range.

Two‑Way Doppler

A DSN station transmits a highly stable uplink carrier, typically at X‑band (7.145–7.190 GHz). The spacecraft’s transponder locks onto the carrier, multiplies its frequency by a coherent turnaround ratio (e.g. $880/749$ for X‑band), and re‑transmits the downlink. The received frequency $f_r$ at the ground station is shifted by the relative line‑of‑sight velocity $v_r$:

$ f_r = f_t \frac{880}{749} \left( 1 - \frac{2 v_r}{c} \right), $

where the factor 2 accounts for the two‑way path. Solving for $v_r$ yields the range‑rate at the epoch of reception, with a precision of better than $0.1\ \text{mm s}^{-1}$ when integrated over 60–1000 s intervals. The Juno mission, for example, exploited this sensitivity to measure Jupiter’s gravity field with exquisite accuracy.

Ranging

The DSN also measures the absolute range by sending a pseudo‑noise (PN) code or a regenerative ranging sequence. The round‑trip light time $\Delta t$ is extracted after calibrating out the spacecraft’s transponder delay $\tau_{\text{s/c}}$:

$ R = \frac{c}{2} \big( \Delta t - \tau_{\text{s/c}} \big). $

Modern regenerative ranging achieves accuracies of 1 m or better, sufficient to anchor the orbit in the radial direction. However, radiometric observables are inherently one‑dimensional: they constrain the line‑of‑sight position and velocity exquisitely, but leave the plane‑of‑sky (lateral) position almost unobservable. A single dish’s beamwidth of $\sim 0.03^\circ$ translates to a lateral uncertainty of 80 000 km at the distance of Mars. This is where interferometry becomes essential.

Delta‑DOR: Interferometric Pinpointing

Delta Differential One‑way Ranging (Delta‑DOR) transforms the DSN into a continent‑scale interferometer, providing angular measurements with nanoradian precision – equivalent to resolving a human hair from 20 000 km away.

Principles of VLBI with a Quasar Reference

Two widely separated DSN stations simultaneously record the downlink signal from the spacecraft. The difference in arrival time, $\tau_{\text{sc}}$, depends on the baseline vector $\mathbf{B}$ (the chord between the stations) and the unit direction to the spacecraft $\mathbf{\hat{s}}$:

$ \tau_{\text{sc}} =\tau_{\text{clk}} + \tau_{\text{atm}} + \tau_{\text{inst}} + \frac{\mathbf{B} \cdot \mathbf{\hat{s}}}{c} $

The extra terms represent station‑clock offsets, tropospheric and ionospheric delays, and instrumental biases. To cancel these common‑mode errors, the observing sequence alternates between the spacecraft and a nearby quasar whose position in the International Celestial Reference Frame (ICRF) is known to better than $10^{-9}$ rad. The quasar’s delay is:

$ \tau_{\text{qso}} = \tau_{\text{clk}} + \tau_{\text{atm}} + \tau_{\text{inst}}+ \frac{\mathbf{B} \cdot \mathbf{\hat{s}}_{\text{qso}}} {c} $

Subtracting the two yields the differential delay:

$ \Delta \tau = \tau_{\text{sc}} - \tau_{\text{qso}} \approx \frac{\mathbf{B}}{c} \cdot \big( \mathbf{\hat{s}} - \mathbf{\hat{s}}_{\text{qso}} \big). $

The angular offset from the quasar is therefore:

$ \Delta \theta \approx \frac{c \Delta \tau}{B \cos \theta}, $

where $\theta$ is the angle between the baseline and the source direction. The quasar acts as a fixed angular reference, removing all station‑based errors to first order. Residual atmospheric delays are calibrated with water‑vapour radiometers and GPS‑based ionospheric maps.

Real Performance

The DSN’s longest baselines are Goldstone–Canberra (10 560 km) and Goldstone–Madrid (8 390 km). With careful calibration, the differential delay can be measured to 30 ps (3 × 10⁻¹¹ s) under favourable observing conditions. For a baseline of 10 000 km and $\cos\theta \approx 1$, this corresponds to an angular resolution:

$ \Delta \theta = \frac{3 \times 10^8 \cdot 3 \times 10^{-11}}{10^7} \approx 9 \times 10^{-10}\ \text{rad} = 0.9\ \text{nrad}. $

At the distance of Mars during the 2020 Perseverance landing (1.7 AU ≈ $2.55 \times 10^{11}$ m), a single Delta‑DOR measurement located the spacecraft to within $2.55 \times 10^{11} \times 9 \times 10^{-10} \approx 230\ \text{m}$ in the plane of the sky. In practice, operational Delta‑DOR accuracies are typically 1–5 nrad; under particularly favourable geometry, as demonstrated during the cruise of the Mars Science Laboratory, sub‑nanoradian performance (0.6 nrad) has been achieved. Juno, Cassini, and BepiColombo have all relied on the technique for precise planetary flybys.

Limitations of Delta‑DOR

Delta‑DOR is not an autonomous method. It requires simultaneous visibility of the spacecraft and a bright, compact quasar from at least two DSN complexes, which imposes scheduling constraints. Solar plasma scintillation can degrade the measurement when the line of sight passes close to the Sun. Most importantly, the technique ties the mission to Earth‑bound infrastructure: the position solution is not available onboard in real time, and a round‑trip command cycle for a spacecraft at Jupiter exceeds 1.5 hours.

Optical Navigation: Autonomous Angular Measurements

For missions requiring onboard autonomy – or operating on the far side of the Sun – optical navigation (OpNav) provides a self‑contained alternative. A star tracker or dedicated navigation camera images a target body (planet, moon, or asteroid) against a background of reference stars. The centroid of the illuminated body is determined to a fraction of a pixel, yielding an angular measurement. If the pixel field‑of‑view (IFOV) is $20\ \mu\text{rad}$ and the centroiding accuracy is $0.1$ pixel, the angular uncertainty is $2\ \mu\text{rad}$. At a range of $10^5$ km, the linear position error in the sky plane is $200\ \text{m}$.

The basic observation equation is:

$ \mathbf{b}_{\text{obs}} = \mathbf{T} \left( \frac{\mathbf{r}_{T}-\mathbf{r}_{\text{s/c}}} {\left|\mathbf{r}_{T}-\mathbf{r}_{\text{s/c}}\right|}\right)+\boldsymbol{\nu} $

where $\mathbf{r}_{T}$ is the target body’s position relative to the Solar System barycentre, $\mathbf{r}_{\text{s/c}}$ is the spacecraft position, $\mathbf{T}$ the camera orientation matrix, and $\boldsymbol{\nu}$ measurement noise. By imaging the same target from different positions along the orbit, the spacecraft can triangulate its trajectory.

Historically, OpNav images were processed on Earth – the Voyager spacecraft, for example, relied on ground‑based reduction of its optical observations to refine trajectories at Jupiter and Saturn. Truly autonomous optical navigation was demonstrated by Deep Space 1; its AutoNav system processed optical images and combined them with an onboard dynamical model to propagate the spacecraft state in real time, forming a complete autonomous navigation loop. Since then, fully autonomous OpNav has been employed by OSIRIS‑REx (which navigated to within a few hundred metres of Bennu without ground intervention) and by DART during its terminal approach to Dimorphos. These missions show that OpNav can serve as the backbone of real‑time, self‑contained navigation near a target body.

XNAV: Pulsar‑Based Navigation

To break free of all Earth and Solar‑system references, researchers have turned to the most stable rotators known: millisecond pulsars. X‑ray pulsar‑based navigation (XNAV) treats these rapidly spinning neutron stars as a naturally occurring, galaxy‑wide positioning system.

Pulsar Timing Model

At the Solar System Barycentre (SSB), the rotational phase of a pulsar can be described by a Taylor series:

$ \Phi_{\text{SSB}}(t) = \Phi_0 + \nu (t - t_0) + \frac{1}{2} \dot{\nu} (t - t_0)^2 + \cdots, $

where $\nu$ is the spin frequency and $\dot{\nu}$ the secular slow‑down rate (typically $10^{-15}$–$10^{-18}$ s⁻²). The phases are so predictable that the SSB arrival time of the N‑th pulse can be predicted years in advance with microsecond accuracy.

A spacecraft carries an X‑ray telescope that records photon arrival times. After folding the photons modulo the pulsar period, a pulse profile is obtained and cross‑correlated with a high‑signal‑to‑noise template to yield a pulse time‑of‑arrival (TOA) at the spacecraft’s proper time $t_{\text{obs}}$. The geometric relationship is

$ t_{\text{SSB}} = t_{\text{obs}} + \frac{\mathbf{\hat{n}} \cdot \mathbf{r}}{c} + \Delta_{\text{rel}} + \Delta_{\text{clock}}, $

where $\mathbf{\hat{n}}$ is the precisely known unit vector to the pulsar, $\mathbf{r}$ the spacecraft position vector relative to the SSB, $\Delta_{\text{rel}}$ the relativistic Einstein and Shapiro delays, and $\Delta_{\text{clock}}$ the offset from a stable onboard timescale. The dominant term, $(\mathbf{\hat{n}} \cdot \mathbf{r})/c$, is the Rømer delay – the light‑travel time across the projection of the spacecraft’s position onto the pulsar line‑of‑sight.

Measurement Precision

The fundamental limit on TOA accuracy for a pulsar with a simple Gaussian‑like pulse of width $W$ is

$ \sigma_{\text{TOA}} \approx \frac{W}{\text{SNR}}, \qquad \text{SNR} \approx \frac{S_p \sqrt{T}}{\sqrt{S_p + S_{\text{bg}}}}, $

where $S_p$ is the pulsed photon count rate, $S_{\text{bg}}$ the combined background rate (diffuse X‑ray sky, detector internal background), and $T$ the integration time.

A concrete, order‑of‑magnitude estimate illuminates the scale: PSR B1937+21, the first millisecond pulsar, has a period of 1.56 ms, a pulsed X‑ray flux of $\sim 2 \times 10^{-13}\ \text{erg cm}^{-2}\text{s}^{-1}$ in the 0.5–10 keV band, and a pulse width of about 10 % of the period ($W \approx 0.156\ \text{ms}$). With an effective area of $2000\ \text{cm}^2$ (representative of NICER’s 56 X‑ray mirrors), the detected pulsed count rate is

$ S_p \approx \frac{2 \times 10^{-13}\ \text{erg cm}^{-2}\text{s}^{-1}}{1.6 \times 10^{-9}\ \text{erg keV}^{-1} \times 1.5\ \text{keV}} \times 2000\ \text{cm}^2 \approx 0.17\ \text{ct s}^{-1}. $

The background (cosmic X‑ray plus internal) is typically $S_{\text{bg}} \approx 1.0\ \text{ct s}^{-1}$. Over an 8‑hour integration ($T = 28800\ \text{s}$), the pulsed counts are $0.17 \times 28800 \approx 4900$ and background counts $\approx 28800$. The SNR is

$ \text{SNR} \approx \frac{4900}{\sqrt{4900 + 28800}} \approx 26. $

The TOA uncertainty then is

$ \sigma_{\text{TOA}} \approx \frac{0.156 \times 10^{-3}}{26} \approx 6.0 \times 10^{-6}\ \text{s} = 6\ \mu\text{s}. $

Multiplying by $c$ gives a line‑of‑sight position error of about 1.8 km. With three or more non‑coplanar pulsars, the 3‑D position can be solved to a level of a few kilometres.

SEXTANT Demonstration

The Station Explorer for X‑ray Timing and Navigation Technology (SEXTANT) aboard the Neutron Star Interior Composition Explorer (NICER) on the International Space Station proved the concept in 2017–2018. Using a carefully selected catalogue of millisecond pulsars (including PSR J0030+0451, J0218+4232, B1937+21, and B1821‑24), SEXTANT achieved fully autonomous, real‑time 3‑D position fixes with an accuracy of 10 km after 8 hours of observation, and 5 km after 2 days. This was the first demonstration that a spacecraft could determine its absolute position in the Solar System without any contact with Earth.

Challenges with XNAV

XNAV is not yet turn‑key for all missions. The major obstacles include:

  • Detector mass and power: A square‑metre‑class X‑ray telescope is heavy and consumes significant power, competing with scientific instruments.
  • Onboard clock stability: An atomic clock is essential; an error of 1 ns maps to 0.3 m of range. The Deep Space Atomic Clock (DSAC), a mercury‑ion trap demonstrated in 2019, exhibits a fractional frequency stability of $<10^{-15}$ over days, corresponding to a time error of less than 1 µs after several days.
  • Pulsar timing noise: Glitches, red noise, and instabilities in the interstellar medium degrade the SSB prediction over time.
  • Integration time: A quick fix still requires several hours of photon collection, which may conflict with mission operations.
  • Sky distribution: Pulsars are weak X‑ray sources, and the brightest usable ones are unevenly scattered on the sky, limiting the instantaneous navigation geometry and requiring careful catalogue selection.

Despite these challenges, XNAV remains the only conceivable method for fully autonomous navigation across the entire Solar System – and perhaps beyond.

Onboard State Estimation

In practice, none of the techniques described above operates in isolation. Every deep‑space spacecraft continuously propagates its state using an onboard dynamical model and, typically, an inertial measurement unit (IMU) or gyroscopes. External measurements – whether radiometric, optical, or pulsar‑based – are periodically fused with this dead‑reckoned state through a navigation filter, almost always an Extended Kalman Filter (EKF) or Unscented Kalman Filter (UKF).

The filter maintains a state vector $\mathbf{x} = [\mathbf{r}, \mathbf{v}, \delta t_{\text{clk}}, \dots]^T$ and a covariance matrix $\mathbf{P}$. Between measurements, the state is propagated using a high‑fidelity force model:

$ \dot{\mathbf{x}} = \mathbf{f}(\mathbf{x}, t) + \mathbf{w}, $

where $\mathbf{f}$ accounts for solar radiation pressure, third‑body perturbations, and thruster firings, and $\mathbf{w}$ is process noise. When an observation $z$ arrives (a pseudorange, a Doppler shift, a quasar‑differential delay, an optical centroid, or a pulsar TOA), the filter computes the innovation $\mathbf{y} = z - h(\mathbf{x}^-)$, the measurement Jacobian $\mathbf{H}$, and updates the state and covariance:

$ \mathbf{K} = \mathbf{P}^- \mathbf{H}^T (\mathbf{H} \mathbf{P}^- \mathbf{H}^T + \mathbf{R})^{-1}, $ $ \mathbf{x}^+ = \mathbf{x}^- + \mathbf{K} \mathbf{y}, $ $ \mathbf{P}^+ = (\mathbf{I} - \mathbf{K} \mathbf{H}) \mathbf{P}^-. $

This continuous fusion of measurements with a dynamical model is what turns a few sparse observations into a coherent, high‑precision orbit solution. Without it, a spacecraft would drift into useless uncertainty within hours. The filter also provides the crucial real‑time covariance that tells the onboard computer – or the ground team – whether the navigation solution is good enough for the next critical manoeuvre.

Most Probable Solution

No single technique rules deep space. Future missions will fuse multiple sources in an onboard Kalman filter, as outlined above. The emerging architecture can be summarised as:

In this vision, Earth‑based tracking provides the “gold standard” for cruise, OpNav takes over when real‑time autonomy is needed near a target, and XNAV fills the gap when the Earth is occulted or too far for timely updates. The LuGRE experiment has already shattered the myth that GNSS cannot work at the Moon, opening the door to a future where lunar orbiters and landers navigate autonomously with metre‑level accuracy. Delta‑DOR, refined over decades, remains the workhorse for planetary flybys. And pulsar navigation, having moved from theory to flight demonstration, points the way to a truly self‑sufficient spacecraft.

Taken together, the techniques described here, from extended‑sensitivity GNSS at the Moon, through Earth‑based radiometric tracking and interferometry, to autonomous optical and pulsar‑based navigation, form the practical toolkit of the deep‑space navigator. Each method addresses a specific range and autonomy requirement, and all are routinely combined within onboard estimation filters to produce the continuous, reliable trajectory knowledge on which every mission depends.
