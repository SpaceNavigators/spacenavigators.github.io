The Real-World Guide to CubeSat Sensor & Actuator Selection

Yajur Kumar December 12, 2025

If you are reading this, you are likely staring at a blank spreadsheet labeled "ADCS Budget" and realizing that the textbooks didn't tell you which part number to buy. I’ve been there. I’ve seen beautiful control laws fail because the sensor noise was higher than the datasheet promised, and I’ve seen missions lost because someone underestimated the magnetic dipole of their own battery cables.

This isn’t a lecture on control theory. This is a guide on how to buy the hardware that will keep your satellite pointing at the ground (or the stars) rather than tumbling aimlessly in Low Earth Orbit (LEO).

1. The Design Requirements: What Do You Actually Need?

Before you open a catalog, you need to lock down your requirements. "Pointing as good as possible" is not a requirement; it’s a recipe for bankruptcy.

In my experience, 90% of CubeSat missions fall into one of these three tiers. Decide which one you are right now.

Tier A: The "Just Survive" (Nadir Pointing / Power Positive)

  • Goal: Keep the solar panels roughly facing the sun and the antenna roughly facing Earth.
  • Accuracy: \(\pm 5^{\circ}\) to \(\pm 10^{\circ}\).
  • Slew Rate: Low (\(< 1^{\circ}/s\)).
  • Typical Payload: Basic comms relay, simple science experiments.

Tier B: The "Look at This City" (Medium Precision)

  • Goal: Take a photo of a specific city or track a large ground feature.
  • Accuracy: \(\pm 0.5^{\circ}\) to \(\pm 2^{\circ}\).
  • Slew Rate: Moderate. You need to slew to targets quickly to maximize ground station time.
  • Typical Payload: Medium-res Earth observation, spectral imagers.

Tier C: The "Pinpoint" (High Precision)

  • Goal: Laser communications, astronomy, or sub-meter ground resolution.
  • Accuracy: \(< 0.1^{\circ}\) (often \(< 0.01^{\circ}\) pointing stability).
  • Slew Rate: High agility or extreme stability.
  • Typical Payload: Star trackers are mandatory here.

2. Numerical Analysis & Sizing (The Physics)

Let’s simulate a design cycle. We will size a 3U CubeSat (approx. \(10 \times 10 \times 34\) cm, 4 kg mass) in a 500 km SSO (Sun-Synchronous Orbit).

You cannot select an actuator until you know your Disturbance Torques. These are the invisible hands trying to spin your satellite.

Step 1: Calculate Your Inertia Matrix

For a 3U CubeSat, we approximate it as a solid rectangular prism.

  • \(Mass (m) = 4.0 \text{ kg}\)
  • \(Dimensions = 0.1 \text{ m} \times 0.1 \text{ m} \times 0.34 \text{ m}\)

$$I_{xx} = I_{yy} = \frac{m}{12} (0.1^2 + 0.34^2) = 0.0418 \text{ kg}\cdot\text{m}^2$$

$$I_{zz} \text{ (Long axis)} = \frac{m}{12} (0.1^2 + 0.1^2) = 0.0067 \text{ kg}\cdot\text{m}^2$$

Note: In reality, your deployable solar panels will change this. Always add a 20% margin to your inertia estimates early in the project.

Step 2: Calculate Disturbance Torques (Worst Case)

We need to know the maximum torque the environment will throw at us.

A. Gravity Gradient Torque (\(T_g\))

This is the Earth trying to pull your long axis towards the center of the planet.

$$T_g = \frac{3\mu}{2R^3} |I_z - I_y| \sin(2\theta)$$

  • \(\mu = 3.986 \times 10^{14} \text{ m}^3/\text{s}^2\)
  • \(R = 6371+500 \text{ km} = 6871 \text{ km}\)
  • Max deviation \(\theta = 45^{\circ}\) (worst case)

$$T_g \approx 1.5 \times 10^{-9} \times (0.0418 - 0.0067) \times 1 \approx \mathbf{5.2 \times 10^{-8} \text{ Nm}}$$

Verdict: Negligible for active control, but useful for passive stabilization.

B. Aerodynamic Torque (\(T_a\))

(A big problem in LEO)

$$T_a = 0.5 \rho V^2 C_d A (cp - cm)$$

  • \(\rho \text{ (Density at 500km)} \approx 2 \times 10^{-13} \text{ kg/m}^3\) (varies wildly with solar cycle!)
  • \(V = 7600 \text{ m/s}\)
  • \(C_d = 2.2\) (Drag coefficient)
  • \(A = 0.034 \text{ m}^2\) (Long side facing velocity)
  • \((cp - cm)\) (Offset between center of pressure and mass) \(= 0.05 \text{ m}\) (Assume 5 cm offset)

$$T_a = 0.5 (2\times 10^{-13})(7600^2)(2.2)(0.034)(0.05) \approx \mathbf{2.1 \times 10^{-5} \text{ Nm}}$$

Verdict: This is your baseline. Your wheels need to hold this constantly.

C. Magnetic Disturbance (\(T_m\))

If your satellite has a residual dipole (from batteries, current loops), Earth's field grabs it.

$$T_m = D \times B$$

  • \(D = 0.1 \text{ Am}^2\) (Typical poor wiring hygiene in CubeSats)
  • \(B = 45,000 \text{ nT} = 4.5 \times 10^{-5} \text{ T}\)

$$T_m = 0.1 \times 4.5 \times 10^{-5} = \mathbf{4.5 \times 10^{-6} \text{ Nm}}$$

Step 3: Sizing the Actuators

Torque Requirement:
To stay stable, your Reaction Wheels (RW) must generate significantly more torque than the disturbances. Total Disturbance \(\approx 2.5 \times 10^{-5} \text{ Nm}\). However, you also need Agility (Slew Rate). To rotate \(90^{\circ}\) in 60 seconds:

$$\alpha = \frac{2\theta}{t^2} = \frac{2(\pi/2)}{60^2} \approx 0.00087 \text{ rad/s}^2$$

$$T_{slew} = I \alpha = 0.0418 \times 0.00087 \approx 3.6 \times 10^{-5} \text{ Nm}$$

Total Required Torque: \(\approx 0.1 \text{ mNm}\) (safe margin).

Momentum Saturation (The Trap):
Aerodynamic torque is constant in one direction for half the orbit. It builds up momentum in the wheels.

$$h_{build} = T_{aero} \times \text{Time} = 2.1 \times 10^{-5} \times (3000 \text{ sec}) \approx \mathbf{63 \text{ mNms}}$$

Crucial: If your wheel only holds 30 mNms, it will saturate (reach max speed) halfway through the orbit, and you will lose control. You need Magnetorquers to "dump" this momentum.

3. The Hardware: A Market Overview

Now that we have the numbers (\(0.1 \text{ mNm}\) torque, \(>60 \text{ mNms}\) momentum), let's look at what you can actually buy.

Actuators

1. Reaction Wheels (RW)
  • A Fair Choice: Sinclair Interplanetary (now Rocket Lab) RW-0.03
    • Stats: 30-40 mNms momentum, 2 mNm torque.
    • My Take: The industry workhorse. Extremely reliable. However, for our 3U calculation above, 30 mNms is too small if drag is high. You would need to dump momentum constantly.
  • The Heavy Lifter: CubeSpace Gen2 Small/Medium
    • Stats: Ranges from 17 mNms to ~100+ mNms.
    • My Take: For a 3U in 500km orbit, I’d look at a wheel offering at least 50-80 mNms. CubeSpace wheels are robust and integrate well with their own ADCS stacks.
  • GomSpace NanoTorque
    • Stats: Integrated 4-wheel pyramid units.
    • My Take: Great for volume saving (compact), but fixing a single failed wheel inside a pyramid block is impossible.
2. Magnetorquers (MTQ)

You must have these to desaturate your wheels.

  • Torque Rods: Usually fit inside the rails of the chassis.
    • Requirement: We calculated a magnetic disturbance of \(4.5 \mu\text{Nm}\). To fight this and dump momentum, you need a dipole of at least \(0.2 \text{ Am}^2\).
  • Options: ISISpace, GomSpace, and CubeSpace all offer rods. Pro tip: Check the "Residual Dipole" of the rod itself. A magnetized core can ruin your magnetometer readings.

Sensors

1. Sun Sensors (Coarse vs. Fine)
  • Coarse (Photodiodes): Every CubeSat needs 6 of these (one on each face). They tell you "Sun is generally that way."
    • Cost: <$100 (if you build them).
  • Fine Sun Sensors:
    • Option: CubeSpace CubeSense or Lens R&D BiSon64.
    • Specs: \(<1^{\circ}\) accuracy, radiation hardened.
    • Use Case: If you don't have a star tracker, this is your primary reference vector.
2. Star Trackers (Precision: If you neeed it!)

If you need \(<0.1^{\circ}\) pointing (Tier C), stop looking at sun sensors and buy this.

  • Blue Canyon Nano Star Tracker (NST)
    • Specs: ~4 arcseconds accuracy.
    • Experience: The benchmark for high-performance missions.
  • CubeSpace CubeStar
    • Specs: Small form factor, \(\sim 0.02^{\circ}\) accuracy.
    • Experience: A solid performer for 3U buses where volume is tight.
3. Magnetometers
  • Usually integrated into the ADCS computer.
  • Warning: Place this as far from your battery and reaction wheels as possible. I once saw a mission where the magnetometer just measured the reaction wheel spinning up. We had to use the torque commands to filter the magnetic data. Don't be that person. Deployable booms are worth the mechanical risk.

5. Some Engineering Advice 

  1. The 'KRAKsat' Loop: You might think power and ADCS are different subsystems, but they are deeply coupled. The KRAKsat mission failed because it entered a tumble. The tumble meant the solar panels couldn't point at the sun, causing voltage to drop. The satellite would die, recharge slightly, boot up, and immediately try to detumble—draining the battery before it could stabilize. It entered a reboot loop (150 times per orbit!) until it died. Lesson: Your ADCS needs a passive "zero-power" or "low-power" mode that allows the batteries to charge before you fire up the torque rods.
  2. The "Lost in Space" Problem: Star trackers are amazing, but they can be blinded by the Sun or Moon. If your Star Tracker is blinded and you don't have Sun sensors as a backup, your satellite is effectively blind. Always have a "Safe Mode" sensor (Sun sensors + Magnetometer).
  3. Wheel Jitter: Big reaction wheels store more momentum, but they vibrate more. If you have a sensitive camera, a poorly balanced wheel will blur your images. Look for wheels with "micro-vibration" specs.
  4. Hardware-in-the-Loop (HITL): Do not launch code you haven't tested on the real hardware. Simulate the orbit, feed the sensors fake data, and watch the wheels spin on your desk. If it acts weird on the desk, it will act weird in space.
